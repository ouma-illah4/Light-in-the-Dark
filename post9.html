Be Still

In the beginning, there was no melody—only the murmur of formless waters and the deep sigh of emptiness. Yet over that void, the Spirit of God moved, waiting, hovering like breath before speech. Then came the Voice. “And God said…” The sound that shattered silence brought shape to the shapeless, rhythm to disorder, cosmos out of chaos. Every mountain, every tide, every pulse of creation began as obedience to that divine command. From stillness came sound, and from sound—order, light, and life. The Voice of God was not thunder for destruction but word for creation, summoning peace out of confusion, purpose out of the abyss.

So it is with the heart of man. Our days churn with anxiety and fear; the waters roar within our minds. We stand like Israel at the edge of the Red Sea—behind us, enemies; before us, impossibility. Yet even then, the Eternal whispers, “Stand still, and see the salvation of the Lord” (Exodus 14:13). The people trembled as the waves crashed and the chariots thundered closer, but God was not hurried by human panic. He parted the sea not with frenzy, but command. In the stillness of faith, the waters obeyed, and a path appeared where there had been none. To be still, then, is not weakness; it is worship. It is the refusal to let fear dictate motion. When we cannot see the way, stillness becomes the highest form of trust.

Stillness bears its own harvest. It is the soil in which peace and clarity grow. When we cease striving, we make room for God to act. Scripture sings this wisdom softly: “Be careful for nothing; but in every thing by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known unto God” (Philippians 4:6). This stillness is not indifference—it is surrender. It is the calm that comes when the soul releases control and allows heaven to take its rightful place at the helm. In such peace, the heart learns to see again. Worry loses its weight; perspective returns. The same Voice that spoke over the ancient deep now speaks within the believer, and every storm begins to quiet.

When you present your case before Him in faith, His answer is not always escape, but endurance wrapped in peace. “When thou passest through the waters, I will be with thee; and through the rivers, they shall not overflow thee” (Isaiah 43:2). His peace, which surpasses all understanding, guards the trembling heart. Sometimes He calms the sea; other times He lets the sea rage to cleanse you of fear. The fire may not be removed, but its heat refines. God passes through the storm with His own, and what was meant to destroy becomes the forge of purity and assurance.

To be still in adversity is to grow into maturity. The hand that chastens is the same hand that shapes. Those whom God loves, He disciplines, for He is sculpting sons, not slaves. In the wilderness, Israel murmured and perished, but the faithful learn silence instead of complaint. They know that “all things work together for good to them that love God, to them who are the called according to his purpose” (Romans 8:28). Stillness in suffering is not resignation—it is transformation. It teaches the heart to trust timing more than explanation,

In the silence of surrender, God vindicates His own. Christ Himself stood still before Pilate, silent before accusers, yielding to the will of the Father. “Not my will, but thine, be done” He prayed, and through that obedience, the cross became a throne. After the storm came glory; after the cry, a crown. He sat down at the right hand of the Almighty, bearing the Name above every name. Stillness, therefore, is not defeat—it is divine alignment, the posture of victory before the dawn.

And the end of it all is calling. You are not stilled merely for survival but for service. Out of the tempest you are called to priesthood—to stand as God’s representative among men, a vessel of His peace. Those who have been quieted by His voice become proclaimers of His praise, “that ye should shew forth the praises of him who hath called you out of darkness into his marvellous light” (1 Peter 2:9). The still soul becomes a sanctuary, the storm-tossed heart becomes a herald. To be still is to hear, and to hear is to become—God’s voice made flesh in the world’s confusion.
